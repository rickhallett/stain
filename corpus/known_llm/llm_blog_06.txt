The Death of the Homepage

Let's be honest: nobody visits your homepage anymore.

That's not a criticism — it's an observation. The way people discover, evaluate, and engage with businesses has fundamentally shifted. Your homepage is still important, but it's no longer the front door. It's the lobby. And most of your visitors are entering through side doors you didn't even know existed.

Here's what's actually happening. Your potential customers are finding you through Google snippets, LinkedIn posts, podcast mentions, and Twitter threads. By the time they land on your site, they've already formed an opinion. They're not browsing — they're confirming.

This changes everything about how we should think about web presence.

Not as a destination, but as a network. Not as a brochure, but as an ecosystem of touchpoints.

The companies that understand this are doing three things differently:

They're investing in content that lives where their audience already is. They're treating every piece of public communication as a potential first impression. And they're measuring success not by homepage traffic, but by the quality of the journey that leads someone to say "yes."

But here's the thing — most businesses are still designing for a world where the homepage is the starting point. They pour resources into hero sections, value propositions above the fold, and carefully crafted navigation. Meanwhile, their actual first impression is a LinkedIn comment from their CEO or a three-star review on G2.

The homepage isn't dead. But its role has changed. And the sooner we accept that, the better our digital strategies will become.

The question isn't "what should our homepage say?" It's "what story are people telling about us before they ever see it?"
