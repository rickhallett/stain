Why Most Mentorship Advice Is Wrong

I've been both a mentor and a mentee. And I can tell you with confidence: the way most people approach mentorship is backwards.

The conventional wisdom goes something like this: find someone successful, ask them for coffee, absorb their wisdom, and apply it to your life. It sounds clean and logical. And yet, it almost never works.

Here's why.

Mentorship isn't a knowledge transfer problem. It's a relationship problem. And relationships don't form because you sent a polished LinkedIn message asking someone to be your mentor. They form through shared context, mutual respect, and — crucially — value flowing in both directions.

But here's what nobody tells you about the mentor-mentee dynamic: the best mentors don't give advice. They ask questions. They don't tell you what to do — they help you discover what you already know.

Not prescriptions. Not formulas. Questions.

To be fair, there's a place for direct guidance. When you're truly lost, someone saying "do this, not that" can be a lifeline. But those moments should be rare, not the default mode.

The mentorship model we've inherited is hierarchical, transactional, and one-directional. The mentorship model that actually works is collaborative, organic, and mutual.

Here are three shifts that changed everything for me:

I stopped asking "can you be my mentor?" and started showing up as someone worth investing in. I stopped seeking advice and started seeking perspective. And I stopped treating mentorship as a milestone and started treating it as a practice.

The best mentoring relationships aren't found. They're built. Slowly, intentionally, and with genuine care.

And that makes all the difference.
