The problem with cooking for one person is that recipes don't believe you exist. Everything serves four. Every ingredient list assumes you have a pantry like a French grandmother's and a social calendar that justifies buying a whole bunch of cilantro when you need three leaves.

I've been living alone for two years and I've settled into a rotation of about six meals that I can make without thinking. Pasta with whatever's in the fridge. Rice and eggs. That chicken thigh thing from the Serious Eats recipe I've made so many times I no longer need the recipe. Beans. More beans. And on Fridays, takeaway, which I tell myself I've earned.

My mother calls every Sunday and asks what I've been eating and I lie and say varied and nutritious things. She once mailed me a slow cooker. I used it twice and now it lives on top of the fridge judging me silently.

The thing nobody warns you about cooking for one is the dishes situation. Cooking a real meal generates the same dishes whether you're feeding one person or four but the motivation to wash up is directly proportional to the number of people who might see the mess. When it's just you, the sink fills quietly and without witness.

I've started cooking bigger portions on Sundays and eating them through the week. This is apparently called meal prep and there's an entire corner of the internet devoted to it with matching containers and color-coded charts. I just call it making too much soup.
