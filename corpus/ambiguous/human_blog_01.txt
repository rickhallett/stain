I quit my job on a Tuesday. Not for any grand reason. The coffee machine broke and I realized I'd been tolerating a lot more than bad coffee for a long time.

There wasn't a plan. I had savings for maybe four months and a vague idea about freelancing that I'd been nursing since 2017 without actually doing anything about it. My wife was supportive in the way that meant she was terrified but didn't want to make it about her.

The first week was strange. I kept waking up at 6:30 and reaching for my phone to check Slack. By Thursday I was reorganizing the garage. By the following Monday I'd started cold-emailing people I barely knew, which is not something I'm good at and I hated every second of it.

But it worked. Sort of. Three of the forty-something emails turned into calls. One of those calls turned into a project. The project paid less than my old salary but I did it in my kitchen in my underwear listening to Coltrane and that felt like it was worth something.

I don't have a moral here. I'm not going to tell you to quit your job. Some weeks I still wonder if I should go back. But there's this thing that happens when you stop doing something you've been doing for years on autopilot — you can hear yourself think again. Whether that's good or bad depends on the day.
