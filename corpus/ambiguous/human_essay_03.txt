My grandmother kept a garden that made no sense. Tomatoes next to roses next to some kind of herb she called "the good mint" to distinguish it from the other mint which had apparently wronged her somehow. There was a system but she never explained it and I suspect it was mostly vibes.

She gardened every morning from about 6am to 8am and then again in the evening when the light changed. She didn't wear gloves. Her hands looked like they belonged to the earth itself — cracked, brown, permanently stained at the nail beds. She was not sentimental about this. She just didn't like gloves.

When I was nine she gave me my own row. I planted carrots because they seemed easy. They were not easy. Something ate them. I cried. She said "that's gardening" and gave me a tomato from her row instead.

I think about her hands a lot now that I work on computers all day. My hands are soft and they mostly type and swipe. Hers built something every day that you could eat or smell or look at. I'm not romanticizing manual labor — she had arthritis by sixty and it wasn't charming. But there was a directness to what she did that I miss. Input, earth, water, time, output. No stakeholder reviews. No sprint retrospectives. Just dirt and patience.
