# Source: https://jamesclear.com/marginal-gains | Author: James Clear | Date: 2015

The fate of British Cycling changed in 2003 when Dave Brailsford became performance director. The organization had suffered nearly a century of mediocrity — since 1908, British riders had won just one Olympic gold medal and never won the Tour de France in 110 years. One bike manufacturer refused to sell to them, fearing it would hurt their brand.

Brailsford introduced "the aggregation of marginal gains" — seeking tiny improvements in everything. He explained: "If you broke down everything that goes into riding a bike, then improve it by 1 percent, you will get a significant increase when you put them all together."

The team made countless small adjustments: redesigned bike seats, alcohol-rubbed tires, heated overshorts, lighter racing suits, optimal massage gels, proper hand-washing techniques, ideal pillows and mattresses, and even painting the truck interior white to spot dust particles that could affect bike performance.

The results transformed British cycling history. Within five years, they won 60 percent of cycling gold medals at the 2008 Beijing Olympics. By 2012, they set nine Olympic and seven world records. Bradley Wiggins became the first British Tour de France winner in 2012, followed by Chris Froome's victories in 2013, 2015, 2016, and 2017. From 2007 to 2017, British cyclists won 178 world championships, 66 Olympic or Paralympic golds, and five Tour de France victories.

The mathematical power is striking: improving 1 percent daily for one year yields thirty-seven times better results. Conversely, declining 1 percent daily approaches zero. Small daily choices compound over time into remarkable gaps between consistent improvers and those who don't.

Success isn't about single dramatic moments but accumulating daily choices to do things slightly better. Habits function as compound interest in self-improvement.
