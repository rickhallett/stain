I hired someone with no experience last year. It was the best decision I've made as a founder.

Here's why.

When you hire for experience, you get someone who knows how things have been done. When you hire for curiosity, you get someone who reimagines how things could be done. The difference is transformative, disruptive, and — frankly — terrifying.

But here's the thing most hiring managers won't tell you: the traditional interview process is designed to filter for conformity, not capability. We ask candidates to recite their achievements like a liturgy. We test for pattern-matching, not pattern-breaking.

And yet, the people who build extraordinary things are rarely the ones who check every box on the job description.

Let me be clear — I'm not saying experience doesn't matter. It does. Context, scar tissue, and institutional knowledge are irreplaceable. But we've created a system where a brilliant 23-year-old with a portfolio of side projects and a fire in their belly gets rejected because they don't have "3-5 years of relevant experience."

To be fair, there's risk involved. Hiring unproven talent means more coaching, more patience, and more tolerance for mistakes. But the upside is extraordinary: fresh perspectives, boundless energy, and a loyalty that comes from being given a chance when nobody else would.

The future of hiring isn't about finding people who've done the job before. It's about finding people who will redefine what the job can be.
