Stop Building Features Nobody Asked For

There's a pattern I've seen in every product team I've worked with, and it goes something like this: someone reads a competitor's changelog, panics, and suddenly there's a new feature on the roadmap that nobody requested.

This is how products die. Not with a bang, but with a bloat.

Here's the uncomfortable reality: feature parity is a race to the bottom. When you build because your competitor built, you're not innovating — you're reacting. And reactive product strategy is the fastest path to a confused product, a frustrated team, and a customer base that can't explain what you actually do.

But here's what I've learned building products for the last eight years: the best product decisions are subtractive, not additive.

Not "what should we build next?" but "what should we stop building?" Not "what features are we missing?" but "what features are distracting us from our core value?"

Let me be specific.

The products people love — the ones they recommend to friends, pay a premium for, and refuse to leave — aren't the ones with the most features. They're the ones that do fewer things, better. They have opinions. They make choices. They say no.

To be fair, there are moments when a new feature is exactly what's needed. Customer feedback is real, and ignoring it is arrogance. But there's a difference between listening to your customers and being led by your competitors.

Build for the problem you understand deeply. Say no to everything else.

That's not a product philosophy. That's survival.
