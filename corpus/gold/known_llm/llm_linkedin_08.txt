Remote work isn't failing. Management is.

I know that's a provocative statement. But hear me out.

Every week, another headline declares that remote work "doesn't work." Companies are mandating return-to-office. Executives are lamenting lost culture and collaboration. And the narrative is clear: the remote experiment is over.

But here's the thing — the data tells a completely different story.

Study after study shows that remote workers are as productive, if not more productive, than their in-office counterparts. Employee satisfaction is higher. Attrition is lower. And the talent pool expands from local to global.

So why the backlash?

Because remote work exposes a truth that many leaders aren't ready to face: if your management style depends on physical proximity to function, it was never good management in the first place.

Not visibility. Not monitoring. Not management-by-walking-around. Actual management. Clear goals, measurable outcomes, and trust.

To be fair, remote work isn't perfect. Isolation, collaboration friction, and the blurring of work-life boundaries are genuine challenges. And yet, these are solvable problems — if leaders are willing to invest in solving them rather than retreating to what's familiar.

The companies winning at remote work share three traits: radical transparency in communication, investment in asynchronous tools, and a culture that measures output over hours.

The future of work isn't about where you sit. It's about how you lead.

And that's the conversation we should actually be having.
